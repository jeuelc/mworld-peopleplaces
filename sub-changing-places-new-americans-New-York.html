New York City is one of the worlds great immigrant cities and is home to more than three million immigrants. They come from every part of the world: China, Mexico, Jamaica, the Dominican Republic, Ecuador, Bangladesh, Italy and so on.

When immigrants arrive in New York City, they often cluster among people of a similar background, and in turn, they create ethnic neighbourhoods. These ethnic enclaves are where immigrants can speak in their own language, sell their own foods and establish community organisations and local newspapers.

Ethnic enclaves provide immigrants with opportunities for jobs and socialising with other people of their own culture. Some people worry, though, about how they may also encourage ethnic segregation and possibly prevent immigrants from integrating into American society.

In New York you find Little Italy, Koreatown and Le Petit Senegal. The Chinatown in Manhattan is one of the oldest Chinese enclaves outside Asia.
