What are the dominant religions in your country and where did they come from?

Few things highlight movement across Asia better than the spread of religions. Hinduism and Buddhism emerged from ancient India, for instance, and radiated out to the rest of the region where they quickly became important in cultural and political life.

As these ideas encountered new communities, they were often interpreted differently ‐ or adjusted to suit local needs. This has resulted in lots of different forms of what outsiders often think of as the same religion. Buddhism in Cambodia, Laos, Burma, Thailand and Sri Lanka, for example, is known as 'Theravada'. Buddhism in China, Korea, Japan, Singapore and much of island Southeast Asia is known as 'Mahayana'.

The arrival of Islam and Christianity, as well as the appeal of local 'animistic' religions (the worship of natural entities), added to the changing of ideas too. So in Asia today, while it looks like the same few religions are practised right across the region, in reality there are actually scores, if not hundreds, of distinctive strands of each religion.
